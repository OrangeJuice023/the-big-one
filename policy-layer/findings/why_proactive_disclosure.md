# Why on-request disclosure fails for DRRM records

**Type:** NORMATIVE ARGUMENT, not evidence. This note reasons *from* the
corpus toward a policy conclusion. Everything factual in it is cited to
evidence recorded elsewhere in `policy-layer/`; the argument itself is the
project's own and should be presented as such.

**Ingested:** S7l.

---

## What was already recorded, and what this adds

`corpus/Marikina/marikina_document_register.notes.md` already establishes the
**legal** gap: RA 10121 requires plans and hazard maps to be "publicly
displayed" (IRR Rule 6 §7; §12(c)(10)) but never specifies *online*,
*downloadable*, or *proactively disclosed*. EO 2, s. 2016 then makes
on-request retrieval the residual default for anything not proactively
published. An LGU can therefore satisfy the letter of RA 10121 with a hard
copy at city hall and a functioning request desk.

That argument establishes that on-request disclosure is **lawful**. It does
not establish that it is **adequate**. This note argues it is not, on grounds
specific to the kind of document at issue.

---

## The core distinction: safety records are not accountability records

FOI exists primarily to serve **accountability**. Budgets, contracts,
appointments, asset declarations — the public interest is in the *ability to
scrutinise*, exercised by those who choose to. A request-based channel serves
this well. The requester is typically a journalist, researcher, auditor, or
watchdog; the fifteen-working-day processing window under EO 2 is immaterial;
and the value of the record does not decay if only a few people obtain it.

A DRRM plan is a different kind of document. Its value lies in being **acted
upon in advance by the people it covers**. Evacuation routes, assembly areas,
early-warning protocols, and designated shelters function only if they are
known before the event. A plan that has been lawfully disclosed to eleven
requesters and to nobody else is legally compliant and operationally inert.

Three consequences follow.

### 1. The timing argument

Requests take time; earthquakes do not announce themselves. EO 2 allows
fifteen working days, extendable. Nobody files an FOI request for an
evacuation route during ground shaking. For a document whose entire function
is pre-event familiarity, a channel that operates on a multi-week cycle is
not a channel to the relevant audience.

### 2. The functional argument

RA 10121 §11(b)(1) requires the LDRRMP to be **formulated *and tested***.
Testing means drills. Drills presuppose that participants know what is being
drilled. There is a chain of dependency — plan → awareness → drill → tested
plan — and on-request disclosure severs it at the first link. An LGU can
therefore satisfy the formulation limb, run drills, and still leave the
population without the document that the drills are supposed to rehearse.

Note that the corpus contains a live instance: Pasig's own **Committee on
Disaster Resilience** directed that copies "be also available to them and
**down loadable thru Pasig City website/platform**" precisely so barangays
and constituents could obtain the plan (Committee Report, 7 June 2023, in
`corpus/Pasig/`). The council understood the dependency. The implementation
did not follow.

### 3. The distributional argument — the strongest one

This is the argument that the corpus evidences directly, and it is the one
that should carry the section.

Filing a formal request requires knowing the record exists, knowing which
office holds it, being able to draft a letter, holding a valid government ID,
and — in Pasig's case — attending in person carrying a USB drive of at least
5GB. Each requirement filters the population.

Now set that against **who the plans themselves identify as most at risk.**
From the Pasig Committee Report of 7 June 2023, the consultant identified the
populations for whom the zero-casualty target was framed:

> public schools, temporary shelter/houses of **ISF** [informal settler
> families], and those living along flood prone areas

And in the same hearing, on the constraint governing those same families:

> ISF were under UDHA Law. PUSO had the data and we could not just evict
> unless we had appropriate place to transfer them.

So the record establishes, in one document, that (a) informal settler
families are a priority population for the plan, (b) they cannot be
relocated pre-event, and (c) the plan covering them is obtainable by letter,
government ID, and a personal USB drive presented in person.

**The disclosure channel selects against the population the plan
prioritises.** That is not a hypothetical about access barriers; it is the
city's own risk assessment and the city's own procedure, read together.

The same structure holds elsewhere in the corpus. Marikina's LDRRMP is
available on eFOI — remotely, which is a lower burden than Pasig's — but
still requires literacy, internet access, and knowledge that foi.gov.ph
exists and that the document is there to ask for. Taguig's updated CLUP was
obtained by a **university student** filing through DENR-LMB
(`DENRLMB-761820630189`, September 2025). That is the profile of a
successful requester across this corpus: researchers and students, not
residents of the barangays named in the hazard chapters.

## The counterexample matters: it is achievable

Quezon City publishes the QCDRRMP 2021–2027 and the QC Risk Atlas 2014 as
direct public downloads. Whatever obstacles exist — capacity, cost, version
control, liability caution — one NCR LGU cleared them. Any argument that
proactive publication is infeasible for a Metro Manila city has to explain
QC.

The infrastructure argument is weak on its own terms. Pateros runs a
WordPress site. Taguig maintains its ordinance index on Google Sites — a free
platform. Pasig operates an asset server that already hosts hundreds of
resolutions, including the very resolution adopting the plan it does not
host. The hosting capacity exists in every case; what varies is what gets
put on it.

## Honest counterarguments

The argument above should not be presented without these.

1. **Some content may warrant restriction.** Contingency plans can identify
   critical infrastructure, response-force capabilities, and security
   vulnerabilities. This is a real concern — but it argues for **redacted
   publication**, not non-publication. No LGU in the corpus states security
   as its reason for withholding; none publishes a redacted version either.

2. **Version control.** An LGU may prefer controlled distribution so that
   users hold the current text. This is solved by dating and versioning
   published documents, which is standard practice, not by withholding them.

3. **Liability caution.** An LGU may fear exposure if a published plan is not
   followed in the event. Speculative — no evidence in this corpus — but
   plausible, and it would be worth asking directly.

4. **Capacity.** Real for the smallest LGUs. Pateros, at 1.66 km² and ~67,000
   people, is not resourced like Quezon City. But Pateros's problem is not
   that it published badly; it is that it published nothing, while
   maintaining a portal for other purposes.

5. **We cannot read intent.** This is the most important limit. The corpus
   documents *what* is disclosed and *how*. It contains no evidence of *why*
   any LGU chose its posture. Institutional inertia, paper-era procedure
   carried into a digital era, absence of a legal requirement, absence of
   demand, and deliberate control are all consistent with the observations.
   The paper should not attribute motive.

## What follows for the recommendation

If the paper makes a recommendation — and a policy paper reasonably might —
the defensible form is narrow and procedural rather than sweeping:

**That the RA 10121 IRR be amended to specify the mode of public display for
LDRRMPs, contingency plans, and hazard maps: proactive publication in
downloadable digital form on the LGU's own domain, dated and versioned, with
provision for redaction where specific content warrants it.**

This is deliberately modest. It does not create a new obligation — the duty
to publicly display already exists in Rule 6 §7. It specifies the *mode*,
which is the precise thing the current text leaves open and which EO 2 then
fills with a request-based default. It is the smallest change that would
close the gap this project documents.

Two supporting points worth stating:

- The national agencies already do this. PHIVOLCS publishes the Valley Fault
  System Atlas and operates FaultFinder and HazardHunterPH as open web
  services. The hazard information is proactively disclosed at national
  level; the local plans that operationalise it are not. The asymmetry sits
  within a single information chain.
- One LGU in the sample already complies with the proposed standard without
  being required to. The rule would formalise existing good practice rather
  than invent a new burden.

## Demarcation

For the write-up: this note is **argument**. The corpus is **evidence**. They
should be visibly separated in any paper, with the normative section clearly
marked and the empirical findings standing independently of whether a reader
accepts the recommendation.

A reader who rejects the recommendation entirely should still be able to
accept the finding: that six LGUs exposed to the same modelled hazard diverge
sharply in disclosure posture, in four documented modes, and that the
divergence is not explained by whether DRRM activity is occurring.

---

## A methodological consequence of this argument: we do not use eFOI ourselves

If the argument above is right — that safety records need proactive
disclosure and that on-request channels systematically exclude the
populations the records are meant to protect — it would be inconsistent for
this project to complete its own corpus by filing eFOI requests wherever a
document is missing.

Doing so would demonstrate only that a research team with institutional
standing, time, and knowledge of the correct channel can obtain the document.
That was never in question. It is the least surprising possible finding and
it is not this project's finding. The claim under test is whether the
document is available to the people the plan is meant to serve *without*
those advantages.

So: where a gap in this corpus is of the `access-foi` type, it is left open
and labelled rather than closed by request. See `SYNTHESIS.md` §8 for the
specific items affected. This is a limitation stated as a decision, not
disclosed as an oversight, and it is symmetrical with the project's own
recommendation — a study arguing for proactive disclosure should not quietly
rely on the request-based channel it is critiquing to finish its own dataset.
